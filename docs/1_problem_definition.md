# Design Opportunity & Problem Definition

## Project Title: Rotadex One-Handed Pill Organizer

---

## 1. Problem Statement
For stroke survivors (experiencing hemiparesis or single-sided weakness), elderly individuals, and patients suffering from severe arthritis, standard weekly pill organizers are major barriers to daily independence. Current designs require two hands, fine motor dexterity, and substantial pinch strength to peel open snap-lids, twist child-proof caps, or scoop tiny pills out of deep, sharp-cornered rectangular wells. 

This leads to:
- **Medication Non-Adherence**: Patients skip doses out of frustration or physical inability to access their medicine.
- **Accidental Spillage**: Fumbling with snap-lids often causes the entire organizer to flip, scattering pills.
- **Loss of Autonomy**: Users become dependent on caregivers for basic daily medical management, lowering their quality of life.

---

## 2. Design Opportunity & Objectives
There is a significant design opportunity to create a purely mechanical, non-electronic, affordable assistive device that enables **independent, one-handed medication retrieval**. By moving away from small snap-latches and adopting a unified, high-surface-area button that uses large-muscle groups (like a palm press), we can completely remove the requirement for finger dexterity.

### Key Objectives:
- **Single-Handed Usability**: All steps—positioning, advancing the day, and retrieving the pills—must be achievable using only one hand.
- **High Physical Stability**: The device must remain stationary on a tabletop during operation, resisting tipping or sliding.
- **Ergonomic Scoop Access**: Pills must be delivered to an accessible, curved ramp or cup that allows easy retrieval by sliding a finger, rather than pinching.
- **Visual & Tactile Clarity**: Clear, high-contrast, and tactile labeling for days of the week to aid users with mild cognitive or visual impairments.
- **Affordable & Cleanable**: The device must be simple to manufacture (injection molding or 3D printing) and easy to disassemble and clean to maintain hygiene.

---

## 3. Scope and Constraints
- **Target Audience**: Stroke survivors with hemiparesis, elderly individuals, patients with rheumatoid arthritis, or anyone with limited upper extremity motor control.
- **Physical Envelope**: Desk/tabletop form factor. Must be compact enough to sit on a nightstand (under $20 \times 20 \times 15\text{ cm}$) but heavy/stable enough to not slide.
- **Capacity**: Must hold up to 6 standard-sized capsules or large multivitamins per day.
- **Material Constraints**: Must use food-safe, durable plastics (like ABS, polypropylene, or PETG) and non-slip materials (silicone or TPU) for the base.
- **Regulatory Focus**: Non-invasive, assistive device class. The design focus is on human factors and mechanics rather than electronics.
